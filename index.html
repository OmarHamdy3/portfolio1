Superstore Business Analysis

An end-to-end sales and profitability analysis built on four years of Superstore transactional data (2014–2017) — from raw Excel records to a star-schema data model, a Python cleaning pipeline, and a four-dashboard Power BI suite that turns scattered numbers into decisions leadership can act on.

01 Project Overview

Brief idea: A comprehensive business intelligence project built on the classic Superstore dataset, giving a 360° view of sales, profit, and customer performance across four years of retail activity.

Main objective: Identify underperforming product categories and regions, quantify the impact of discounting on margin, and translate the findings into a reusable, interactive dashboard suite.

Data type: Structured transactional records — orders, returns, customers, products, shipping, and geography.

Key metrics tracked: Total Sales, Total Profit, Profit Margin, Sales Growth (YoY), Discount Impact, Average Order Value, and Customer Lifetime Value (CLV).

02 Business Problem

Leadership could see overall revenue was growing, but had little visibility into which specific segments — products, regions, customer types — were actually profitable. Finance suspected that heavy discounting was eroding margin in certain categories, but the raw data wasn't in a format that supported quick analysis. Decisions on marketing spend and inventory allocation were being made on intuition rather than evidence.

03 Project Plan & Workflow

The project was scoped into five sequential phases, taking the dataset from a raw Excel export to a fully interactive, forecastable dashboard suite.

01
DATA COLLECTION & CLEANING · PYTHON

Prepare a high-quality dataset

Loaded the raw dataset with Pandas, checked for missing values, duplicates, and inconsistencies, then handled gaps (median imputation for Sales & Profit, mean for Quantity & Discount), converted data types, removed unnecessary columns, and screened for outliers before saving a clean working copy.

PandasMissing valuesDuplicatesOutlier detectionType conversion
02
DATA MODELING

Structure the data as a Star Schema

Identified fact and dimension tables, designed a star schema with a central Sales fact table plus dimension tables for Customers, Products, Orders, Time, Location and Ship Mode, then implemented the relationships in Power BI for fast, join-light querying.

Fact tableDimension tablesRelationshipsPower BI model
03
BUSINESS QUESTIONS & EDA

Answer key business questions

Grouped questions into Orders, Customers, Products, Location, Time, and Ship Mode, then ran descriptive statistics and exploratory visualizations with Matplotlib and Seaborn to surface the trends and patterns hiding in four years of transactions.

MatplotlibSeabornDescriptive statsTrend detection
04
SALES FORECASTING

Project future sales trends

Isolated the time-series signal in the cleaned dataset to model seasonality — most notably the recurring November–December peak — as a foundation for forward-looking sales projections inside the dashboard.

05
DASHBOARD DESIGN

Build the interactive reporting layer

Designed four Power BI report templates — Overview, Order Details, Product, and Customer — covering total sales & profit trend, top customers & products, sales by region & ship mode, and forecasted sales, optimized for at-a-glance clarity.

04 Data Cleaning Pipeline (Python)

The cleaning notebook runs as a linear, reproducible pipeline — each step verified before moving to the next.

01

05 Data Modeling — Star Schema

A star schema was chosen over a flat table for one reason: it turns a wide, repetitive export into a queryable model — one fact table for transactions, and lean dimension tables for everything that describes them.

FACT TABLE

Fact_Sales

The central table — every transaction, with foreign keys linking out to each dimension for filtering and grouping.

SalesQuantityDiscountProfitCustomer FKProduct FKDate FKShip Mode FK
Dim_Customers

Customer ID, name, segment, and location for behavioral and geographic slicing.

Dim_Products

Product ID, name, category and sub-category for the product hierarchy.

Dim_ShipModes

Same Day, First, Second, and Standard Class shipping categories.

Dim_Dates

Year, quarter, month, and day attributes for time-based analysis.

Faster queries — fewer, simpler joins improve report performance.
Better readability — an organized structure that's easy to reason about.
Scalability — suited to large datasets and BI tools like Power BI or Tableau.

07 Key Business Insights (2014–2017)

Findings pulled from the exploratory analysis, organized by the same lens as the dashboard suite.

Time-Based Performance
Profit climbed steadily from a 2014 low to a 2017 high, with November and December the strongest months in every year, and January–February consistently the weakest — January 2015 recorded the worst single month at −$3,281.
Quantity sold and order volume both trend upward year-on-year, peaking in 2017, with the same November–December seasonal spike and February slump repeating across the dataset.
Total sales followed the same pattern: 2017 was the strongest year, 2015 the weakest, with November the peak-selling month throughout.
Product & Category Performance
Office Supplies led profit in 2014, but Technology took over from 2015 onward; Phones led sub-categories early, with Copiers overtaking from 2016.
The Canon ImageCLASS 2200 Copier was the single most profitable and highest-grossing product in both 2016 and 2017.
Office Supplies dominated quantity sold and order count every year, with Binders the top-selling sub-category and Staples the top product from 2016.
Regional & State Performance
The West region led on profit, quantity, orders, and sales in nearly every year; the Central region posted the lowest profit in 2014 and 2017.
California was the top-performing state across almost every metric, while Texas underperformed on profit and Wyoming consistently had the lowest sales.
Shipping Analysis
Standard Class was the most profitable, most used, and highest-selling shipping mode across nearly every year and segment — the one exception being Home Office in 2015, where Second Class edged ahead on profit.
Delivery time by mode: Same Day — instant, First Class — 2 days, Second Class — 3 days, Standard Class — 5 days.

01 Project Overview

Brief idea: A data-driven platform that analyzes road accident data to identify high-risk locations, weather conditions, and time patterns. The goal is to provide safety teams with actionable intelligence to reduce accident severity and frequency.

Main objective: Transform a fragmented set of accident records into a centralized, queryable analytics platform that answers the core question: where and when should we intervene?

Data type: Structured historical accident records — date, time, location, weather conditions, severity, and vehicle involvement.

Key metrics tracked: Accident Frequency, Severity Index, Weather Impact Score, Time-of-Day Risk, Location Risk Scores.

02 Business Problem

Raw accident records were inconsistent — location data was messy, weather conditions were often omitted, and there was no easy way to correlate accidents across multiple dimensions (time, location, weather, severity). City planners and safety officers had no clear way to prioritize interventions or allocate resources effectively. Decisions about where to install traffic calming measures or increase patrols were being made on intuition rather than evidence.

03 Analysis Objectives

  • Clean and standardize accident records from multiple sources.
  • Identify the top 10 high-risk accident locations (by severity and frequency).
  • Analyze the correlation between weather conditions and accident severity.
  • Determine peak times for accidents (by hour, day, season).
  • Create an interactive dashboard for safety teams to explore data on their own.

04 Dataset Description

Key columns: Accident ID, Date, Time, Location (Lat/Long, City, Road Type), Weather Conditions (Temperature, Visibility, Precipitation), Severity (Fatal, Serious, Slight), Number of Vehicles Involved.

Data quality issues identified: Missing location coordinates (geocoded using City/Road info), inconsistent severity categories (mapped to a 3-level scale), incomplete weather data (backfilled using a weather API).

07 Key Business Insights

Location & Geospatial
20% of intersections accounted for 45% of severe accidents, indicating a clear need for targeted infrastructure improvements.
The Highway 12 corridor had the highest risk score — requiring immediate attention.
Residential streets had the highest volume of slight accidents, while highways had the highest proportion of fatal accidents.
Time-of-Day & Seasonality
Accidents peaked during evening rush hour (5–7 PM) and late-night hours (midnight–3 AM).
Nighttime accidents (10 PM – 6 AM) were twice as likely to be fatal, suggesting a need for better lighting and visibility measures.
Winter months (December–February) had the highest accident volume, with a secondary peak in July (holiday travel).
Weather Correlation
Rainy conditions were associated with a 30% increase in accident frequency, particularly on highways.
Low visibility (fog, heavy rain) correlated with higher severity — accidents in these conditions were 45% more likely to be fatal or serious.
Temperature had a weaker correlation, but icy conditions (below 0°C) spiked accident rates in the early morning hours.
Severity & Vehicle Involvement
Accidents involving heavy vehicles (trucks, buses) had a 4× higher fatality rate than those involving only passenger cars.
Single-vehicle accidents were the most common type, but multi-vehicle collisions had the highest severity.
